Integrated Tolerance and Drift Budget for Concave-Mirror Multipass Gas Cells Under Laboratory, Field, and Drone Environments
IEEE-style technical paper report
Prepared for multipass/Herriott gas-cell mechanical design comparison
ABSTRACT
This paper-style report develops an integrated tolerance and drift budget for concave-mirror multipass gas cells. The analysis compares metal, Bosch-rail, plastic, and hybrid mirror-mount concepts for laboratory, field, and drone deployment. The central correction adopted here is that construction tolerance is a recoverable manufacturing/fixing error if the cell has adequate capture range, while final in-use tolerance is dominated by residual alignment and operational drift. Operational drift is separated into independent position drift Pxy and independent angular drift Thetaxy. The combined spot-pattern variation is then calculated only as a final optical-clearance metric using the mirror spacing. The report includes physical constants, assumptions, equations, cost and weight indices, material references, and all figures from the previous integrated comparison.
Index Terms—multipass gas cell, Herriott cell, tolerance budget, concave mirror, kinematic mount, flexure mount, plastic optics mount, drone sensor, thermal drift, vibration drift.

I. INTRODUCTION
Compact multipass gas cells are highly sensitive to small mechanical changes because the optical pattern makes repeated passes between concave mirrors. A small mirror tilt or input-beam angular change can move the pattern by tens to hundreds of micrometres. This is manageable in a laboratory cell that can be realigned, but becomes critical in field and drone instruments where vibration, temperature cycling and pressure changes occur after alignment.
The purpose of this document is to compare practical construction routes rather than only ideal optical designs. It therefore includes conventional machined aluminium, stainless steel, titanium, Invar inserts, Bosch-rail prototypes, 3D-printed plastic, CNC-milled engineering plastic, injection-moulded plastic with inserts, and hybrid plastic body with aluminium mirror cartridge. Plastic is retained throughout the analysis because low cost and low mass may be attractive, particularly for high-volume sensors. However, plastics generally have higher coefficient of thermal expansion, lower modulus, creep and larger vibration response than metal references [3]-[8].
The previous report style is preserved by keeping the practical comparison tables, block diagram, mount concept illustration, total spot-position charts, total angle charts, cost comparison and final recommendations. The content is now organized as an IEEE-style paper with equations and references.
II. ASSUMPTIONS AND PHYSICAL CONSTANTS
The calculation is intentionally a first-order engineering budget. It is not a finite-element analysis and it is not a substitute for vibration testing. Each value is estimated from a transparent physical relationship, a material property, or a conservative process factor. The purpose is to rank design concepts and identify which concepts are likely to remain inside an acceptable spot-pattern envelope.

III. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
Fig. 1. Integrated tolerance calculation block diagram. Construction error is checked against capture range, but the final in-use drift is computed from residual alignment and operational drift.
The error budget is written as a six-degree vector. Construction error is not automatically added to final drift because it can be removed by alignment, provided the mount has enough capture range. The final post-alignment drift vector is:
T = Ralign + Dop = [dx, dy, dz, dθx, dθy, dθz].
The input spot-position component and the input angular component are deliberately reported separately:
Pxy = sqrt(dx^2 + dy^2),     Θxy = sqrt(dθx^2 + dθy^2).
Only when evaluating the internal Herriott spot clearance is the angular term converted into a spot movement at the opposite mirror:
Xangle = L × Θxy / 1000.  For L = 300 mm, Xangle = 0.3 × Θxy, where Θxy is in mrad.
Therefore, the reported combined spot-pattern variation is:
Xcombined = Pxy + Xangle = Pxy + 0.3 × Θxy.  This is a conservative maximum-drift envelope, not an RMS statistical uncertainty.
A. Construction tolerance estimation
Construction tolerance is the maximum manufacturing/fixing accuracy before optical alignment. The value is estimated from the process route: CNC-machined metal with dowel pins gives the smallest positional and angular error, Bosch rail has larger joint slip and orthogonality error, 3D printing has anisotropy and larger dimensional scatter, and injection moulding has high repeatability only after tooling is correct. Construction error is used as a capture-range check. If the alignment system can steer the beam/mirror far enough, the construction error is recoverable.
For a mirror pair, position construction error is reduced to a radial equivalent Pbuild = sqrt(dxbuild^2 + dybuild^2). Angular construction error is reduced to Θbuild = sqrt(dθx,build^2 + dθy,build^2). These quantities are listed in the construction table and are compared against the available alignment range.

B. Alignment tolerance and user capability
Alignment tolerance is the maximum residual left after the user aligns the input and output optics. It is not the same as construction tolerance. It depends on actuator resolution, layout sensitivity, feedback quality, user experience, access to screws, whether the detector signal is visible during alignment, and whether the source/detector are monolithically integrated. The practical model used is:
Ralign = Fresolution × Fease × Fuser × Ffeedback.
Easy alignment represents a visible beam or strong detector signal, good screw access, adequate range and a low-sensitivity layout. Medium alignment represents a compact cartridge with limited access but usable feedback. Hard alignment represents dense spot patterns, indirect detector feedback, multiple coupled adjustments or high vibration constraints.

C. Operational drift estimation
Operational drift is the non-recoverable change after alignment during use. It is caused by thermal expansion, vibration, pressure loading, epoxy/hardware relaxation and thermal cycling. For the final maximum-drift budget, the position and angle components are added as absolute maximum component changes rather than statistical RSS terms.
Thermal position drift is estimated from ΔxT ≈ kT,L × Lref × |αstructure − αreference| × ΔT. The coefficient kT,L represents how much of the free thermal expansion becomes optical decentre; it is small for symmetric metal flexures and large for asymmetric plastic or rail assemblies. Thermal angular drift is estimated from ΔθT ≈ kT,θ × |αsupport − αmirror| × ΔT, with larger kT,θ for non-isostatic clamps, continuous adhesive, or plastic seats.
Vibration drift is estimated through a compliance factor rather than a detailed modal model: stiff monolithic/flexure metal references have low values, kinematic mounts and rail assemblies have higher values, and plastics have higher values because of lower modulus and damping/creep. Pressure drift is estimated from window/end-cap deflection and seal compression; O-rings are treated as seals only. Cycling drift captures screw relaxation, epoxy creep, plastic creep, retainer-set and return hysteresis after repeated thermal or vibration cycles.

IV. MOUNT CONCEPTS AND DESIGN OPTIONS
Fig. 2. Concave mirror mount concepts. The figure compares machined aluminium flexure and pocket mounts, 3D-printed plastic, CNC-milled engineering plastic and injection-moulded plastic with inserts.
Five practical mount families dominate the design space. A machined aluminium isostatic flexure mount gives the best balance of mass, manufacturability and drift for drone use. A monolithic aluminium pocket mount is cheaper but gives higher tilt drift because the mirror plane is less decoupled from the cell body. 3D-printed plastic is lowest cost and lowest mass but has the highest drift risk. CNC-milled engineering plastic improves geometry and material control. Injection-moulded plastic with metal inserts is attractive for high-volume low-cost production but requires careful tool design, metal inserts and local reference surfaces.

VI. HOW EACH NUMERICAL VALUE WAS ESTIMATED
Every numerical value in Table VII follows the chain below. This section is included so that the values can be changed later when measured values or FEA results become available.
--- TABLE ---
Reported value | Estimation basis | How the value was chosen
dx, dy construction | From process capability and datum quality | CNC metal/dowel pins: 25-100 µm; Bosch rail/plastic: 100-500 µm; 3D printed plastic: high due to print anisotropy and post-processing scatter
dz construction | From axial pocket depth, end-cap machining and seal compression | Metal pocket/cartridge: 50-200 µm; plastic/rail: larger because datum and seal compression are less controlled
dθx, dθy construction | From mirror seat flatness, clamp symmetry and datum separation | Approximate angular error = seat height error / datum radius; e.g., 20 µm over 40 mm ≈ 0.5 mrad
dθz construction | From hole/clocking/dowel angular accuracy | Important for off-axis mirror holes because roll moves the hole relative to the pattern by Rhole × dθz
Alignment residual | From component resolution × ease factor × user factor × feedback factor | Easy/medium/hard regimes represent practical residuals after alignment; kinematic mounts can resolve small changes but user feedback limits final residual
Thermal position drift | CTE mismatch × length × asymmetry factor | Low for symmetric metal flexures; high for plastic and Bosch rail because α and joint/asymmetry factors are larger
Thermal angular drift | CTE mismatch × temperature swing × clamp/flexure sensitivity | Invar and titanium reduce this; plastic seats and epoxy rings increase it
Vibration drift | Compliance/risk factor based on stiffness, mass, joints and locking | Flexures and monolithic blocks are low; external kinematic mounts, rails and plastics are high for drone use
Pressure drift | End-cap/window/seal compliance and O-ring compression | O-rings are allowed only as seals; if they define the mirror plane, pressure drift becomes a primary error
Cycle/hysteresis drift | Return error after thermal/vibration cycling | Higher for plastic creep, screw relaxation, retaining rings and continuous epoxy
Pxy | sqrt(dx^2+dy^2) | Position drift magnitude in the equivalent input plane
Θxy | sqrt(dθx^2+dθy^2) | Input-angle drift magnitude; kept separate from position drift
Angle-induced spot | L × Θxy / 1000 | For L=300 mm and Θ in mrad, angle-induced spot = 0.3Θ
Combined spot | Pxy + angle-induced spot | Conservative maximum optical clearance metric

Example calculation: drone aluminium isostatic flexure
The drone aluminium isostatic flexure case has Pxy = 0.184 mm and Θxy = 0.424 mrad. With L = 300 mm, the angular term produces Xangle = 0.3 × 0.424 = 0.127 mm. The combined spot-pattern variation is therefore Xcombined = 0.184 + 0.127 = 0.311 mm. The construction error for this concept is Pbuild = 0.071 mm and Θbuild = 0.141 mrad. It is marked recoverable because the expected alignment capture range is larger than the construction error. The remaining final drift is therefore due to residual alignment plus thermal/vibration/pressure/cycling effects, not because the part was initially machined with zero error.
VII. DISCUSSION
A. Laboratory design consequence
For laboratory development, alignment flexibility is more valuable than ruggedness. External kinematic mounts on machined metal are acceptable because the system can be realigned and vibration is low. Bosch rail and 3D printed mounts may be useful for early mock-ups but should not be used as final optical references if quantitative gas-cell stability is required. Plastic can be used in laboratory prototypes, but it should not be interpreted as a proof that the same design will survive field or drone operation.
B. Field design consequence
For field use, construction error is still mostly recoverable if the cartridge has enough alignment range. The dominant risk becomes return drift after transport, pressure changes and temperature cycling. Aluminium flexure, stainless cartridge, titanium cartridge and Invar local mirror insert are all technically acceptable. Injection-moulded plastic with inserts may be acceptable only if the spot-pattern clearance is relaxed or if metal optical references are added.
C. Drone design consequence
Drone use is the most demanding case because vibration converts compliance into angular drift. Normal external kinematic mounts and Bosch rail references are not recommended. All-aluminium isostatic flexure is the best cost-weight-performance compromise for a drone-only design. Titanium improves drift-to-weight further but increases cost. Invar is best thermally but is usually too heavy if used as the full structure. Plastic is attractive for low cost and mass, but pure plastic optical references produce high drift; the recommended low-cost route is a hybrid plastic outer body with an aluminium mirror cartridge.
D. Plastic route assessment
3D-printed plastic is best treated as a prototype route. CNC-milled engineering plastics such as PEEK, PEI or POM are better because surfaces, bores and dowel references can be machined accurately. Injection-moulding is attractive when unit cost matters, but tool shrinkage, fibre orientation if filled grades are used, insert creep, and O-ring compression must be designed carefully. The safest product-grade plastic concept is therefore not an all-plastic optical datum, but a hybrid architecture: plastic housing plus metal mirror cartridge, metal inserts, locating bosses and an O-ring used only for sealing.

IX. VALIDATION PLAN
The numbers in this report should be validated by progressively replacing estimates with measurements. Recommended tests are:
Room-temperature construction and alignment capture test: verify that the actual construction error is recoverable by the available adjustment range.
Thermal sweep test: record spot pattern or detector signal over the expected lab/field/drone temperature swing.
Vibration test: excite the cell with the expected drone frequency content and measure equivalent input-angle drift and spot-position drift separately.
Pressure cycling test: vary the internal cell pressure and verify that the mirror plane is not defined by O-ring compression.
Thermal cycling and ageing test: repeat alignment after cycling to measure creep, retainer set, epoxy relaxation and hysteresis.
Plastic-specific test: compare printed, milled and injection-moulded mounts under identical thermal/vibration conditions before selecting a low-cost route.
X. CONCLUSION
The main conclusion is that construction tolerance is not the same as final optical tolerance. Construction error can be mitigated by alignment if the design has enough capture range. The final in-use tolerance is the residual alignment vector plus operational drift from thermal expansion, vibration, pressure and cycling. Theta is treated as a true independent input-angle drift, while Pxy is treated as independent input spot-position drift. For laboratory systems, external kinematic mounts are acceptable. For field systems, lockable metal cartridges are preferred. For drone systems, aluminium or titanium isostatic flexure cartridges are preferred. Plastic is feasible for low-cost designs only when used carefully, preferably as a housing or low-cost body combined with local metal mirror references.
