Post-alignment tolerance and operational drift analysis for concave-mirror multipass gas cells
Updated framework: construction tolerance is recoverable by alignment when sufficient range and feedback are available
Prepared for gas-cell design comparison under laboratory, field and drone operating conditions. Values are first-order engineering estimates for a 300 mm mirror separation and should be verified by ray tracing, thermal FEA and vibration testing before final release.
[Heading 1] Executive summary
Yes, construction/fixing tolerance can be mitigated by alignment. It should therefore be treated primarily as an alignment-range and manufacturability check, not automatically as final in-use drift.
The final usable spot-pattern variation after commissioning comes from residual alignment error plus operational drift: thermal expansion, vibration, pressure-induced deformation, and thermal cycling/repeatability.
A poor construction method can still fail even if it is alignable: if it drifts under temperature, pressure or vibration, the aligned spot pattern will move during operation.
The best drone/field design remains a dowel-pinned, monolithic or isostatic flexure architecture. External kinematic mirrors can compensate construction error in the lab but are not stable enough for drone operation.
[Heading 1] 1. Definitions used in this update
--- TABLE ---
0 | 1 | 2
Construction tolerance | Manufacturing/fixing accuracy: mirror dx, dy, dz and dtheta x/y/z before final optical alignment. | Used to check whether the alignment system has enough capture range. Not included directly in final drift if it is aligned out.
Alignment tolerance | Residual setpoint error after the user aligns the cell. | Depends on adjuster resolution, feedback visibility, coupling between axes, and user capability.
Operational drift | Post-alignment movement during use. | Includes thermal change, vibration, pressure loading and thermal cycling. This is the dominant real in-use tolerance after alignment.
Maximum total variation | Worst-case excursion from the intended post-alignment setpoint. | Conservative model: residual after alignment + operational drift. RSS is also shown for comparison.
--- END TABLE ---
[Heading 1] 2. Updated theoretical framework: construction tolerance can be aligned out
Figure 1. Updated calculation logic. Construction tolerance is used first as a recoverability/capture-range check.
The previous conservative model added construction manufacturing error directly into the total. That is only valid for a fixed, non-adjustable cell. For an adjustable or jig-aligned multipass cell, the better model is:
1. Convert construction errors into equivalent input spot and angle errors: C_eq.
2. Check whether the alignment mechanism can correct them: C_eq <= alignment capture range.
3. If recoverable, replace C_eq by the smaller post-alignment residual R_align.
4. Add operational drift D_op from thermal, vibration, pressure and thermal cycling.
5. Calculate maximum total variation after alignment: X_total,max = R_align + D_op. Equivalent angle: theta_eq = X_total,max / L.
For L = 300 mm, the conversion used is:
theta_eq [mrad] = X_eq [mm] / 0.3. Therefore 0.1 mm equivalent input spot variation is approximately 0.333 mrad input angle variation.

[Heading 1] 4. Alignment regimes and capture-range logic
Alignment has two different meanings: capture range and final residual. A hard-to-align lab setup may have a very large capture range because two folding mirrors can steer widely, but the final repeatability is worse. A monolithic drone cell has smaller capture range but much better residual alignment stability.
--- TABLE ---
Alignment regime | Typical layout | Capture range eq. spot (mm) | dx,dy residual after alignment (mm) | dz residual after alignment (mm) | dtheta input residual (mrad) | Eq. input spot residual at M2 (mm) | Interpretation
Easy | Monolithic source/collimator, dowel-pinned detector, visible pilot beam/camera feedback | 0.2 | ±0.030 | ±0.050 | 0.080 | 0.038 | Best residual; requires good manufacturing
Medium | Dowel-pinned fibre collimator, shim, one folding mirror or compact cage | 0.6 | ±0.080 | ±0.150 | 0.250 | 0.11 | Best field/lab compromise
Hard | Two external folding mirrors, free-space source/detector, weak internal feedback | 2.0 | ±0.200 | ±0.300 | 0.600 | 0.269 | Large range but poor repeatability; lab only

[Heading 1] 5. Operational drift after alignment
Once the cell is aligned, the main tolerance becomes drift. Drift comes from four terms: temperature, vibration, pressure loading and thermal cycling. These terms move the already-aligned mirror/input/output references and cannot be removed unless the system is re-aligned or actively controlled.
--- TABLE ---
Operational drift term | Physical source | Design implication
Thermal drift | CTE mismatch, mirror-carrier expansion, epoxy stress, end-plate bending | Aluminium and plastic references drift more than Ti/Invar/flexure references.
Vibration drift | Mount compliance, screw slip, rail motion, dynamic bending | External kinematic mirrors and Bosch rail references are weakest.
Pressure drift | Cell wall/end-plate deformation and O-ring compression under pressure variation | Metal-to-metal optical references with O-ring only as a seal are preferred.
Thermal cycling drift | Hysteresis after repeated hot/cold cycles, epoxy creep, clamp relaxation | This is why drone/field cells should be locked, pinned, flexure-supported and vibration tested.
--- END TABLE ---

[Heading 1] 9. Practical design rule
Use construction tolerance as a capture-range criterion: the as-built mirror/source/detector error must be small enough that the alignment procedure can recover the correct spot pattern.
Use alignment tolerance as the residual error after the user has finished aligning, not as the full construction error.
Use operational drift as the real in-use tolerance after alignment. This includes thermal drift, vibration, pressure deformation and thermal cycling/hysteresis.
For drone and field cells, the most important question is not Can I align it once? but Will it stay aligned after vibration, pressure and thermal cycling?
[Heading 1] 10. Validation checklist before final design freeze
--- TABLE ---
Test | Purpose
Construction capture | Build one cell at worst-case machining tolerance and confirm the alignment procedure can recover the nominal spot pattern.
Thermal drift | Cycle over the expected lab/field/drone temperature range and record spot-pattern displacement or throughput loss.
Vibration drift | Apply representative vibration/transport or drone vibration and measure pre/post alignment shift.
Pressure drift | Vary pressure over operating range and measure beam shift/throughput change.
Thermal cycling repeatability | Repeat hot/cold cycles and record permanent offset after returning to room temperature.
Locking validation | After final alignment, lock/stake screws or bond flexures, then repeat thermal/vibration tests.
--- END TABLE ---
