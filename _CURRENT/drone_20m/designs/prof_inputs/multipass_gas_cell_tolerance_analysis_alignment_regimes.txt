Revised Multipass Gas Cell Tolerance, Alignment Capability and Maximum Drift Analysis
Concave mirror construction, alignment component resolution, and operational thermal/vibration drift
Prepared for: Thomas Benoy
[Heading 1] Terminology used in this revised report
Construction tolerance = manufacturing and fixing accuracy. This is the maximum as-built x/y/z and angular error of the two concave mirrors. The mirror position and tilt values are kept explicitly in the construction tables.
Alignment tolerance = practical residual alignment capability. It starts from component resolution/repeatability and is multiplied by alignment difficulty and user capability. It does not include lab/field/drone thermal or vibration assumptions.
Operational drift = maximum added movement under the relevant environment after construction and alignment. It includes temperature-driven CTE mismatch, O-ring/epoxy/joint creep risk, and vibration-induced angular/position movement.
All effects are converted into equivalent input spot-pattern variation in mm and equivalent input angle in mrad using L = 300 mm mirror spacing.
[Heading 1] Executive summary
The practical design target is not just machining accuracy; the final gas cell must keep the spot pattern stable after temperature and vibration exposure.
At 300 mm mirror spacing, 0.1 mrad mirror tilt produces approximately 60 micrometres of spot shift on the opposite mirror; 0.1 mm equivalent input spot variation corresponds to about 0.33 mrad equivalent input angle.
Best laboratory route: aluminium/stainless cell with external low-drift kinematic mirror mounts and two folding mirrors for alignment freedom.
Best field route: aluminium body with titanium/stainless/Invar mirror cartridge, dowel-pinned input collimator, and fixed output detector/focusing module.
Best drone route: titanium or Invar-insert isostatic mirror cartridge, monolithic source/collimator, and direct 2 mm detector or monolithic micro-lens detector. Avoid external folding mirrors, Bosch rail references and O-ring-defined mirror planes.
In this report, maximum tolerance for the destination is ordered by operational severity: drone has the largest allowable/expected total drift envelope, then field, then laboratory. This is different from manufacturing accuracy, where drone construction should be the tightest.

[Heading 1] 1. Assumptions and conversion rules
Table 2. Model assumptions.
--- TABLE ---
Quantity | Value used | Comment
Mirror spacing, L | 300 mm | Scale all angle-to-spot values linearly with L
Mirror tilt conversion | dx_spot = 2 L dtheta_mirror | Reflection doubles mirror angular error
Input angle conversion | dx_spot = L dtheta_input | Input beam angle directly propagates to next mirror
Axial construction effect | dx_z approx 0.1 dz | First-order sensitivity used for compact Herriott geometry
Lab temperature excursion | 5 K | Controlled laboratory/enclosure
Field temperature excursion | 25 K | Transport/field deployment
Drone temperature excursion | 40 K | Outdoor payload plus electronics heating
Direct detector beam diameter | 0.5 mm | Used for direct detector margin
Focused beam diameter | 0.2 mm | Used for lens/OAP/micro-lens cases
--- END TABLE ---
Equivalent input spot from any angular error is reported at the cell input reference, so construction, alignment and operational errors can be compared on the same axis.
For dense spot patterns, the preferred internal maximum spot variation is roughly <=0.20 mm for lab, <=0.10 mm for field, and <=0.05 mm preferred / <=0.10 mm practical for drone.
dtheta_z roll is retained in the tables because a mirror hole at 15 mm radius moves by about 15 micrometres per 1 mrad roll, even though pitch/yaw dominate the Herriott pattern.
[Heading 1] 2. Block diagram and theoretical framework for total tolerance
The total tolerance calculation is performed by converting every physical error source into the same input-equivalent coordinates: equivalent input spot variation in mm and equivalent input angle theta in mrad. Construction, alignment and operation can then be combined consistently.
Figure 1. Block diagram of the total tolerance calculation. All mechanical, alignment and operational effects are first converted to equivalent input spot and theta before being combined.
Table 3. Theoretical framework for total tolerance calculation.
--- TABLE ---
Term | Meaning | What it contains | Conversion/use
Construction term | Manufacturing and fixing accuracy | Mirror dx, dy, dz, dtheta x/y/z | Converted to equivalent input spot using lateral error plus 2L mirror tilt term
Input alignment term | Practical ability to set input beam | Component resolution, alignment difficulty, user capability | dx_input and L dtheta_input, multiplied by ease/user factor
Output alignment term | Practical ability to collect exiting beam | Detector/lens/mirror setting resolution and detector margin | Converted into equivalent input spot demand and compared with detector area
Operational drift term | Temperature and vibration after build/alignment | CTE mismatch, epoxy/O-ring creep, mount vibration, frame slip | Converted to equivalent input spot/angle and added as maximum drift
Total design value | RSS design budget | Root-sum-square of independent maximum terms | Used to rank likely performance
Conservative value | Worst-case arithmetic sum | All terms move in same direction | Used to size mirror-hole and detector margins
--- END TABLE ---

--- TABLE ---
User capability level | Operator multiplier used
Expert optical engineer | 0.8
Trained photonics user | 1
Field technician | 1.5
General user / production operator | 2.5
--- END TABLE ---
Practical alignment tolerance is not only the screw resolution of a kinematic mount. It also depends on whether the user can observe the spot pattern, whether x/y and angle are coupled, whether the adjusters are accessible, and whether locking the mount changes the setting.
The model used here is: practical_alignment = resolution_only * component_difficulty_multiplier * operator_capability_multiplier.
A monolithic source/detector has less user alignment freedom but a smaller practical residual because the alignment is set by machining and assembly fixtures. Two folding mirrors provide high flexibility but more coupled degrees of freedom and poorer vibration survival.

Table 7A. Practical alignment regimes. The numbers are maximum residual errors after alignment and should be selected according to how easily a real user can align the input/output chain.
Use the easy regime when the source, collimator and detector are monolithically referenced to the gas-cell body or dowel-pinned with clear optical feedback. Use the medium regime for shimmed or compact kinematic modules. Use the hard regime when two external folding mirrors or several independent free-space components must be aligned only by throughput. In the total-tolerance framework, the selected regime replaces the ideal resolution-only value before RSS combination with construction and operational drift.

Laboratory: the best choice is external kinematic mounts and two folding mirrors because alignment freedom is more important than vibration survival.
Field: the best choice is a lockable or isostatic cartridge. Use dowel pins for x/y/clocking and screws only for clamping. O-rings should seal outside the reference path.
Drone: the best choice is monolithic alignment. Do not use external folding mirrors as part of the final alignment chain. Use a titanium mirror cartridge or local Invar reference insert with three-dot epoxy/flexure support.
For 1 mm detector output, many configurations can fail the collection margin after drift. A 2 mm detector or focused/micro-lens detector is much safer mechanically.
[Heading 1] 9. Maximum destination tolerance envelope
Here tolerance means the maximum total spot-pattern/angle variation that the final cell design should be able to accommodate for the intended destination. Therefore the operational tolerance envelope is intentionally largest for drone, followed by field, then lab. This does not mean drone manufacturing accuracy is looser; it means the drone cell must be designed with more drift margin while still using tighter construction and alignment methods.

Lab maximum tolerance envelope: 0.20 mm total equivalent input spot variation, corresponding to 0.67 mrad equivalent input theta for L = 300 mm.

For dense Herriott spot patterns, the mirror-hole clearance and detector collection margin must be designed to survive the destination tolerance envelope, while the construction and alignment methods should still aim for the lowest practical drift.
[Heading 1] 10. Manufacturing and validation checklist
Check construction tolerance first: measure mirror seat x/y/z, mirror pitch/yaw and roll before optical alignment.
Then check alignment tolerance: record the smallest repeatable change possible using the chosen input and output alignment components.
Then check operational drift: thermally cycle and vibrate the complete assembled cell, not just the mirror mount, and convert observed spot shift to equivalent input angle using dtheta_input = dx_spot / L.
Record both RSS design variation and worst-case arithmetic variation. Use the worst-case value for safety margin around mirror holes and detector active area.
For epoxy, use three small symmetric dots on a stress-relieved carrier or flexure. Avoid a continuous glue ring and avoid using O-ring compression as the optical datum.
[Heading 1] References and engineering notes
1. Typical CTE values are taken from standard engineering material data ranges: aluminium ~23 ppm/K, stainless ~16 ppm/K, titanium ~8.6 ppm/K, Invar ~1-2 ppm/K, fused silica ~0.55 ppm/K.
2. Optical epoxy is modelled with high CTE relative to fused silica/metal; EPO-TEK 353ND-style epoxies are suitable only when the bond geometry is small and symmetric.
3. O-rings are treated as seals only. Elastomer compression should not define mirror plane angle or mirror axial position.
